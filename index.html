Cross-sectional Study
1. A cross-sectional study was conducted to assess the burden of asthma. Out of 2,000 individuals screened at a specific point in time (snapshot), 150 were found to be currently suffering from the disease. What is the prevalence of the disease?
  • a) 15.0%
  • b) 7.5%
  • c) 13.3%
  • d) 0.075%
  • e) 1.5%
Why this formula?
A cross-sectional study measures exposure and outcome at the same time. Therefore, it is used to measure Prevalence of disease (existing cases).
Prevalence of disease = (Cases / Total population) × 100
Calculation:
Cases = 150
Total Population = 2,000
Prevalence = (150 / 2000) × 100 = 7.5%
Cross-sectional Study
2. In the same cross-sectional study of 2,000 individuals, participants were surveyed about their habits. 400 individuals reported being current smokers. What is the prevalence of exposure?
  • a) 15%
  • b) 20%
  • c) 25%
  • d) 30%
  • e) 40%
Why this formula?
Cross-sectional studies also measure the proportion of the population exposed to a certain risk factor at that specific snapshot in time.
Prevalence of exposure = (Exposed / Total population) × 100
Calculation:
Exposed individuals = 400
Total Population = 2,000
Prevalence of exposure = (400 / 2000) × 100 = 20%
Cohort Study
3. A prospective cohort study follows a group of 5,000 healthy, disease-free individuals. After a 5-year follow-up period, 250 new cases of diabetes are diagnosed. Calculate the incidence of the disease.
  • a) 50%
  • b) 20%
  • c) 5%
  • d) 0.5%
  • e) 2.5%
Why this formula?
A cohort study follows a disease-free population over time to see who develops the disease. It measures Incidence (new cases over a period).
Incidence of disease = (New cases / Population at risk) × 100
Calculation:
New Cases = 250
Population at risk = 5,000
Incidence = (250 / 5000) × 100 = 5%
Cohort Study
4. In a cohort study investigating the effect of air pollution on lung disease, the results showed that the incidence of lung disease among the exposed group was 20%, while the incidence among the non-exposed group was 2%. Calculate the Relative Risk (RR).
  • a) 0.1
  • b) 18
  • c) 22
  • d) 10
  • e) 40
Why this formula?
The primary measure of association in an observational cohort study is the Relative Risk (RR). It compares disease incidence in the exposed vs. non-exposed.
Relative Risk (RR) = Incidence in exposed / Incidence in non-exposed
Calculation:
Incidence in exposed = 20%
Incidence in non-exposed = 2%
RR = 20 / 2 = 10
Case-Control Study
5. A case-control study compared patients with uterine cancer (Cases) to healthy individuals (Controls) and asked about past Tamoxifen use. Based on the data below, calculate the Odds Ratio (OR):
Uterine Cancer (Cases) No Cancer (Controls)
Took Tamoxifen (Exposed) 139 (a) 58 (b)
Did NOT take (Non-Exposed) 550 (c) 953 (d)
  • a) 1.50
  • b) 2.40
  • c) 4.15
  • d) 0.24
  • e) 10.60
Why this formula?
A case-control study starts with the disease and looks backward (retrospectively). We estimate risk using the Odds Ratio (OR).
Odds Ratio (OR) = (a × d) / (b × c)
Calculation:
OR = (139 × 953) / (58 × 550)
OR = 132,467 / 31,900 = 4.15
Clinical Trial
6. A randomized controlled trial tested a new vaccine. The incidence of the disease in the treatment group (receiving the vaccine) was 2%. The incidence in the control group (receiving a placebo) was 10%. Calculate the Relative Risk (RR) in this interventional study.
  • a) 0.2
  • b) 0.5
  • c) 2.0
  • d) 5.0
  • e) 8.0
Why this formula?
In clinical trials, RR compares the outcome in the treated group to the outcome in the control group. A value less than 1 indicates the treatment is protective.
Relative Risk (RR) = Incidence in treatment group / Incidence in control group
Calculation:
Incidence in treatment = 2%
Incidence in control = 10%
RR = 2 / 10 = 0.2
Clinical Trial
7. In a clinical trial to prevent stroke, the incidence of stroke in the control group was 15%. The incidence of stroke in the treatment group receiving the new drug was 10%. Calculate the Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR).
  • a) 1.5%
  • b) 5%
  • c) 10%
  • d) 25%
  • e) 50%
Why this formula?
ARR calculates the simple absolute difference in risk between the control group and the treatment group to show the actual risk removed by the drug.
Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) = Incidence in control group - Incidence in treatment group
Calculation:
ARR = 15% - 10% = 5%
Clinical Trial
8. Using the data from the previous stroke trial, where the Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) was calculated to be 5% (or 0.05). Calculate the Number Needed to Treat (NNT) to prevent one stroke.
  • a) 5
  • b) 10
  • c) 15
  • d) 20
  • e) 50
Why this formula?
NNT is a clinical metric. It tells the physician exactly how many patients must be treated with the intervention to prevent one additional bad outcome.
Number Needed to Treat (NNT) = 1 / ARR
Calculation:
ARR = 0.05
NNT = 1 / 0.05 = 20
